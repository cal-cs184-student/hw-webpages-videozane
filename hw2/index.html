Section I: Bezier Curves and Surfaces

Part 1: Bezier curves with 1D de Casteljau subdivision

De Casteljau's algorithm is a recursive method to evaluate Bezier curves. Given a set of $n$ control points and a parameter $t$ (where $0 \leq t \leq 1$), the algorithm computes $n-1$ intermediate points by linearly interpolating between adjacent control points.

In my evaluateStep function, I loop through the given vector of points and apply the standard linear interpolation formula to each adjacent pair:
$$P_i' = (1 - t)P_i + tP_{i+1}$$
I store these new interpolated points in a new vector and return it. This represents one single step of the algorithm.

Part 2: Bezier surfaces with separable 1D de Casteljau

The 1D de Casteljau algorithm extends naturally to 2D Bezier surfaces by evaluating the surface as a grid of Bezier curves. Given a 2D grid of control points $(n \times m)$ and two parameters $(u, v)$, we can evaluate the surface in two passes.

In my evaluate function, I first iterate through each of the $n$ rows of control points. For each row, I treat it as a standalone 1D Bezier curve and fully evaluate it at parameter $u$ using my evaluate1D helper function. This reduces the 2D grid of points down to a single column (a 1D vector) of evaluated points. Finally, I evaluate this remaining column at parameter $v$ to find the final 3D point on the surface.

Section II: Triangle Meshes and Half-Edge Data Structure

Part 3: Area-weighted vertex normals

To implement smooth shading, I calculated the area-weighted normal at a given vertex by averaging the normals of all its neighboring, non-boundary faces.

I traversed the neighboring faces by iterating through the half-edges originating from the central vertex. For each face, I grabbed the coordinates of its three vertices. I computed two edge vectors by subtracting the central vertex position from the other two vertices. Taking the cross product of these two vectors gives a vector orthogonal to the face. Because the magnitude of a cross product is proportional to the area of the parallelogram formed by the two vectors, simply adding these cross products together automatically weights them by the area of their respective triangles. Finally, I called .unit() on the summed vector to normalize it.

Part 4: Edge flip

To implement the edge flip operation, I carefully mapped out the local neighborhood of the edge on a piece of paper, labeling every half-edge ($h0$ to $h5$), vertex ($v0$ to $v3$), edge ($e0$ to $e4$), and face ($f0, f1$).

If the edge was a boundary edge, I returned immediately to avoid invalid operations. Otherwise, I collected pointers to all elements in the two adjoining triangles. The actual "flip" does not require creating or deleting any elements; it strictly requires reassigning pointers. I systematically called setNeighbors on all six half-edges to rotate their vertex and edge assignments. Finally, I updated the half-edge pointers for the associated vertices, edges, and faces to ensure they pointed to a valid half-edge in the new topology.

Debugging Journey: By strictly mapping out the before-and-after states on paper and ensuring I reassigned every pointer (even if it seemed like it hadn't changed), I managed to avoid major bugs and keep the mesh topology completely stable.

Part 5: Edge split

Unlike an edge flip, an edge split requires creating new mesh elements. Splitting two adjacent triangles yields four triangles. This requires 1 new vertex, 3 new edges, 6 new half-edges, and 2 new faces.

I first collected all the existing elements exactly as I did in the flip operation. I then allocated the new elements using the appropriate allocation methods. I positioned the new vertex at the exact midpoint of the original split edge by averaging the positions of its two endpoints. Next, I used setNeighbors across all 12 half-edges (6 old, 6 new) to wire them into the four new triangular loops. Finally, I updated the half-edge pointers for all vertices, edges, and faces. I also set the isNew flags for the newly created edges, which was critical preparation for the Loop subdivision logic in Part 6.

Part 6: Loop subdivision for mesh upsampling

I implemented Loop subdivision by breaking it down into five distinct passes over the mesh:

  1. Compute new positions for original vertices: I iterated through all original vertices. Using the Loop subdivision weighting rule, I computed their new positions based on their degree and the sum of their neighboring vertex positions. I stored this in Vertex::newPosition and marked isNew = false.
  2. Compute positions for new vertices: I iterated over all original edges and calculated the position of the vertex that would eventually be created upon splitting. I stored this in Edge::newPosition.
  3. Split original edges: I iterated through a pre-saved list of original edges. For each, I called splitEdge. I assigned the newly created vertex's position to the value I stored on the edge in Step 2, and marked the vertex as isNew = true.
  4. Flip new edges: I iterated through all edges. If an edge was marked isNew and it connected exactly one old vertex and one new vertex, I flipped it to correct the topology.
  5. Update positions: Finally, I iterated through all vertices, updating their actual position data with the values stored in newPosition.

Observations on sharp corners: As Loop subdivision is applied, sharp edges and corners become significantly smoothed out, pulling the mesh inward and shrinking its overall volume. To reduce this effect, we can pre-split edges near sharp features. By adding more geometry (vertices) near a corner before subdividing, the area-weighted averaging has less influence over the original corner vertex, causing the sharp feature to be preserved much better.

Cube Asymmetry: When subdividing cube.dae, it predictably becomes slightly asymmetric. The original cube mesh consists of exactly two triangles per face, meaning each square face is split by a single diagonal edge. Because Loop subdivision relies on the topological degree of vertices, vertices that have the diagonal attached have a different degree (and therefore a different weighting) than vertices without it. This uneven pulling causes the shape to warp asymmetrically.

The Fix: By pre-processing the cube—specifically by splitting the single diagonal on each face so that every face has a symmetric "X" across it—we ensure that all 8 corners of the cube have the exact same topological degree. When subdivided, the weighting is perfectly symmetrical, resulting in a symmetrical, smoothed cube.
